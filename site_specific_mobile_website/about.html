Stone Enclosure: Rock Rings (1977-78) by Nancy Holt

Stone Enclosure: Rock Rings (1977-78) by Nancy Holt is a significant sculptural work located at Western Washington University. This piece marks Holt's first foray into stone masonry, utilizing the ancient Brown Mountain Stone from British Columbia. Designed by Holt and executed by stonemason Al Poynter, the sculpture consists of two concentric stone rings measuring 40 and 20 feet in diameter. These rings are meticulously aligned with the North Star, reflecting Holt's deep interest in celestial navigation and its historical and metaphorical implications.

The sculpture incorporates four arches that run north to south, calculated from the North Star, which holds significance for both coastal navigators and the local community of Bellingham. "Stone Enclosure" resonates as a sacred space, blending elements of architecture, sculpture, and landscape. Its circular holes, oriented towards different compass points, extend the viewer’s vision outward, integrating the landscape and celestial movements into the viewer’s experience.

Holt's work invites layers of interpretation, drawing connections between the celestial alignments of Stonehenge, ancient amphitheaters, and Native American medicine wheels. This architectural space not only anchors itself deeply in geological history with its 230-million-year-old schist rocks but also engages the spectator in a larger cyclical passage of time, encouraging a profound interaction with the surrounding landscape.
